The Anaconda Standard
Anaconda, Mont. — Wednesday Morning, January 17, 1906

Crippled Man Tires of Living

Believing Wife to be Unfaithful, Edward Keister Shoots Himself

Great Falls, Jan. 16 — Having been partially paralyzed for more than four years, and believing his wife had been guilty of intimacy with another man, Edward Keister, aged 57 years, for 33 years a resident of Northern Montana, committed suicide at 5 o'clock this morning at his home in Choteau county, roughly 210 miles east of this city. Sitting up in bed, he held a .45 caliber revolver back of his right ear and sent a bullet into his brain, falling back lifeless on the pillow.

Keister, who was a wealthy stockman, was injured four years ago last summer. While driving across Bell Creek on a load of poles, the load was blown over and fell on him, causing partial paralysis. After receiving treatment here, he went to eastern hospitals, but did not improve in health and about two months ago, he returned to Montana.

Begins Divorce Suit — A few days after his return he began suit for divorce from his wife, who had been in charge of all his property, alleging too great intimacy with Harry Watts, a ranch hand in her employ. He also alleged that she had converted to her own use his property, valued at $50,000 and had refused to contribute to his support. He secured an injunction.

preventing her from disposing of any more of the property and it was restored to his possession. A few days later, he brought suit against Watts for damages of $15,000, alleging alienation of his wife's affections.

He Takes Possession — After bringing the suits, Keister took possession of the Highwood Ranch, placing Mr. and Mrs. John Jacoby and family in charge while he took up his home there. Monday night, he informed Mrs. Jacoby that he was tired of the litigation and would go to Fort Benton in the morning to meet his wife and settle their troubles. He then seemed cheerful as usual. He slept in the room adjoining that which was occupied by the Jacobys. At 5 o'clock in the morning, the Jacobys were awakened by a shot and, going to Keister's room found him dead in his bed. Beside him lay the revolver from which the shot had been fired, which he had borrowed recently from his attorney at Fort Benton. The coroner held an inquest in the afternoon, the jury finding a verdict of suicide.

Mr. Keister is survived by his wife, to whom he was married 23 years ago, and by four children ranging in age from 8 to 23 years. He was born in Missouri.
